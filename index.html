    A Code of Professional Ethics for AI Developers in Reproductive Embryo Selection Systems

    Translating medical professionalism into operational, enforceable obligations for the engineers who design and deploy artificial intelligence in in vitro fertilization and preimplantation genetic testing.

    Section 01 · Preamble

    Purpose, scope, and audience.

    This Code of Ethics provides ethical guidance for artificial intelligence developers who design and deploy machine learning systems for embryo selection, ranking, and polygenic risk scoring in in vitro fertilization (IVF) and preimplantation genetic testing (PGT) contexts.

    Similar to the professional oaths and bioethical principles that guide physicians, this document outlines the professional duties of AI engineers in reproductive medicine. Although these developers do not practice medicine, the systems they build directly influence clinical decisions regarding embryo selection and the well-being of future children.

    To ensure that innovations in IVF and PGT are deployed ethically and safely, AI developers shall subscribe to fiduciary standards of patient welfare, scientific integrity, and social responsibility.

    Section 02 · Foundational Commitments

    Six commitments that define the developer's role.

    These overarching duties mirror the core principles of medical professionalism and establish the moral foundation for AI developers working in reproductive medicine. Each commitment is expandable.

    I.
    Responsible Embryo-Selection System Design

    Developers shall place the welfare of patients and future children above all competing interests. No commercial objective or organizational pressure may override the developer's responsibility to avoid harm and act in the best interest of the patient.

    II.
    Transparent Presentation of Embryo Risk Scores

    Developers shall be honest and forthright about their AI system's capabilities and limitations. Polygenic risk scores and embryo rankings shall be presented as probabilistic — not deterministic — predictions. Developers shall meet this obligation by publishing a Model Summary documenting confidence intervals, predictive validity across populations, and relevant reproductive outcome benchmarks. Where proprietary constraints limit disclosure, developers shall obtain an independent third-party audit.

    III.
    Support for Informed Reproductive Counseling

    Developers shall design and release AI systems in a manner that enables clinicians and genetic counselors to accurately communicate what embryo risk scores do and do not represent. Outputs shall be interpretable, and accompanying documentation shall clarify intended use, limitations, and contraindications to support meaningful informed consent.

    IV.
    Equity in Polygenic Prediction

    Developers shall actively promote fairness and equity in the design and deployment of AI systems. Developers shall audit algorithms for population-specific performance disparities, document predictive validity across ancestry groups represented in training data, and refrain from deploying systems that perform unequally without disclosed mitigation strategies.

    V.
    Scientific and Technical Integrity

    Developers shall ground their work in sound science and accepted engineering practices. This includes reproducible model development, documented validation against retrospective datasets, calibration testing, and stability analysis. Developers shall not deploy systems for clinical use without evidence of technical performance appropriate to the intended use-case.

    VI.
    Independence from Market Pressures

    Developers shall identify and disclose conflicts of interest — whether from employers, partner organizations, or funders — that could compromise ethical judgment. Ethical decisions concerning embryo-selection systems shall be insulated from commercial incentives that might encourage premature deployment, overstated claims, or the concealment of known limitations.

    Section 03 · Lifecycle Obligations

    Four phases. Four sets of operational duties.

    The six commitments are translated into concrete obligations mapped to each stage of the AI development pipeline. Select a phase to see the corresponding obligations.

    Phase 01 · Data & Model Design
    Ground the system in representative data.
    Use representative datasets. Developers shall use datasets representative of the populations in which the system is intended to be deployed.
    Document provenance. Ancestry composition, clinical source, and known gaps in coverage shall be documented.
    Justify trait inclusion. Inclusion of traits shall be justified by medical benefit — prevention of serious genetic disease or clinically meaningful reproductive outcomes — not by marketing appeal.
    Phase 02 · Validation & Testing
    Validate against what developers can control.
    Retrospective validation. Developers shall validate systems against retrospective datasets prior to release.
    Calibration & reproducibility. Calibration testing, reproducibility analysis, and subgroup stability checks shall be performed and documented.
    External validation. External validation across clinical sites and patient groups shall precede deployment.
    Clinical-outcome validation is shared. Validation against clinical outcomes such as live birth rate is a shared responsibility, contingent on authorized data-sharing arrangements with clinical partners.
    Phase 03 · Deployment & Use
    Equip clinicians to use the system responsibly.
    Role-specific training. Developers shall require and document training prior to use — for clinicians, embryologists, and genetic counselors — covering interpretation of uncertainty, contraindications, and common misuse patterns.
    Human oversight. Systems shall be deployed with an appropriate human-oversight model, empirically evaluated for its effect on outcomes.
    Regulatory clearance. No system shall be deployed clinically without ethical or regulatory clearance.
    Marketing restraint. Marketing and presentation shall not overstate the model's capabilities.
    Phase 04 · Monitoring & Maintenance
    Track technical performance; partner on clinical outcomes.
    Drift monitoring. Developers shall monitor technical performance drift post-deployment.
    Periodic public reporting. Developers shall publish periodic updates on subgroup performance and corrective actions.
    Withdrawal thresholds. Systems shall be suspended or withdrawn when minimum performance or equity thresholds are not met.
    Clinical outcome monitoring is shared. Clinical outcome monitoring remains the responsibility of the deploying clinician; developers shall support this work where authorized data-sharing permits.
    Section 04 · Prohibited Practices

    Practices incompatible with the Code.

    The following practices are strictly prohibited under this Code. Each prohibition is paired with a brief rationale grounded in the foundational commitments.

    Prohibition · 01

    Ranking embryos by non-medical traits

    Rationale. Ranking embryos by traits unrelated to the prevention of serious genetic disease or clinically meaningful reproductive outcomes risks commodifying future persons, reinforces social stratification, and exceeds the clinical mandate of reproductive medicine.

    Prohibition · 02

    Deployment without documented validation

    Rationale. Marketing or clinical integration without documented validation criteria and performance benchmarks exposes patients to unquantified risk and violates standards of scientific integrity and non-maleficence.

    Prohibition · 03

    Deterministic claims about probabilistic outputs

    Rationale. Representing probabilistic polygenic scores as deterministic predictions or guarantees undermines informed consent and distorts reproductive decision-making.

    Prohibition · 04

    Concealment of known limitations

    Rationale. Obscuring known limitations in training-data composition, population-specific predictive gaps, or material model instability perpetuates inequity and prevents clinicians from appropriately contextualizing risk.

    Prohibition · 05

    "Research-only" labeling used to evade oversight

    Rationale. Labeling systems as "research-only" while knowingly facilitating clinical decision use circumvents regulatory safeguards and shifts unacknowledged risk onto patients and clinicians.

    Section 05 · Accountability & Oversight

    Mechanisms that make the Code enforceable.

    Ethical obligations are credible only insofar as they can be documented, audited, and acted upon. The following mechanisms support enforcement within developer organizations and across the profession.

    Published Model Summary

    Developers shall maintain and publish a Model Summary documenting validation datasets, calibration results, subgroup performance, intended use-cases, and known limitations.

    Independent third-party audit

    Where proprietary constraints prevent full disclosure, developers shall commission independent technical audits at defined intervals and disclose summary findings to clinical partners and regulators.

    Conflict-of-interest disclosure

    Developers shall maintain disclosures covering employer, organizational, and funder relationships, and update them when material changes occur.

    Suspension and withdrawal protocols

    Developers shall define, in advance, the performance and equity thresholds that trigger suspension or withdrawal of a deployed system, and shall document the decision process when such thresholds are reached.

    Interdisciplinary ethics review

    Developers shall seek interdisciplinary review — involving clinicians, bioethicists, and affected communities where feasible — at key decision points in the development lifecycle.

    Appendix · Rationales & Literature

    Sources grounding the Code.

    The foundational commitments draw on established bioethics scholarship, existing medical professionalism frameworks, and empirical evidence regarding AI in reproductive medicine.

    Commitment I · Responsible Embryo-Selection System Design

    Grounded in the ABIM Physician Charter (2002), which identifies patient welfare as the cornerstone of medical professionalism. Cohen et al. (2025) documents how AI tools in reproductive medicine remain clinically unproven and may mislead patients if adopted prematurely.

    Commitment II · Transparent Presentation of Embryo Risk Scores

    The ASRM Ethics Committee (2025) highlights how polygenic scores in PGT are frequently overstated, reinforcing the need for clear communication of predictive limits. Munsie & Hyun (2014) describes stem-cell tourism as a cautionary precedent in which opacity enabled exploitation and overhyped therapeutic claims.

    Commitment III · Support for Informed Reproductive Counseling

    Griffin et al. (2024) shows that developers frequently underestimate downstream effects of their systems on patient autonomy when outputs are not interpretable. Koplin et al. (2025) and Aufieri & Mastrocola (2025) document how limited transparency impairs informed consent and patient decision-making.

    Commitment IV · Equity in Polygenic Prediction

    Haining et al. (2025) raises concerns about racial disparities in polygenic risk score predictive validity. Koplin et al. (2025) supports the need for professional norms to protect against bias in reproductive AI.

    Commitment V · Scientific and Technical Integrity

    Thirumalaraju et al. (2025) provides direct evidence of model instability and reproducibility problems, illustrating how premature deployment undermines scientific rigor. Cohen et al. (2025) and Haining et al. (2025) argue for stronger regulatory frameworks and evidentiary thresholds before deployment.

    Commitment VI · Independence from Market Pressures

    Munsie & Hyun (2014) documents how commercial incentives in the stem-cell industry produced an ethical collapse, emphasizing the need to insulate ethical judgment from commercialization pressures.

    Limitations

    Stakeholder consultations were informal. The Code has not yet been empirically validated through formal stakeholder review — for example, a Delphi survey — or tested for adoption in industry or professional society settings. These are among the next steps for the project.
